1 What is Microsoft 365 Copilot?

Microsoft 365 Copilot is your AI assistant woven into the apps you already use — Outlook, Teams, Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and Copilot Chat. It works over your emails, meetings, files, and calendar (securely, inside your Microsoft 365 tenant) so the help you get is grounded in your real work, not generic answers.

3 How to get the most from any prompt

Use the / key to link your content. Anywhere a prompt shows something in [brackets], that's where you insert a live link: type / in Copilot and pick the executive's mailbox, a file, a meeting, or a person. Replacing the placeholder with a real link is what lets Copilot pull the right inbox, calendar, or document — and keeps it clear which mailbox the answer came from.
Be specific about the output. Tell Copilot the format you want ("a table", "under 150 words", "three bullet points"), the tone ("warm", "executive-ready"), and the audience. The more direction you give, the better the result.
It's a conversation, not a vending machine. If the first draft isn't quite right, just say "make it shorter," "more formal," or "add the budget numbers." Copilot remembers the context of your chat.
Keep it professional and confidential. Treat Copilot like a trusted assistant. Use real context, but follow your normal judgment on sensitive information.

Executive Admins

A day supporting a Ford leader

These prompts follow one morning-to-evening arc for an executive admin — starting the day, getting your leader ready, drafting on their behalf, coordinating logistics, and closing the loop. Run them in order to see how Copilot supports the whole flow, or copy any one on its own.

Before you run these: anything in [brackets] is where you drop in a live link — in Copilot, type / and choose the executive's mailbox, a file, or a meeting. Because each prompt points at one mailbox, the results stay clear about whose inbox or calendar they came from. For the inbox and calendar prompts you'll need delegate or shared access to the executive's mailbox.

1 Start the day: triage the inbox

It's 8 AM. Before your leader logs on, you want to know what landed over the weekend and what needs attention.

Outlook / Copilot Chat
Review the inbox for [the executive] and summarize the emails that arrived since Friday, focusing on anything high importance or time-sensitive. Sort them into three groups: needs the executive's attention, needs my follow-up, and FYI only. For each email, give me a one-sentence summary, who it's from, and what it's asking for.

2 Look ahead: this week's calendar

Now you scan the week so there are no surprises — gaps, conflicts, or meetings missing an agenda.

Copilot Chat
Review the calendar for [the executive] for this week and summarize each meeting, including its purpose, the key attendees, and whether it already has an agenda. Flag any double-bookings, any meetings without a clear purpose, and any days that are fully back-to-back with no break.

3 Get them ready: build a meeting brief

There's an important meeting today. You put together a one-page brief so your leader walks in prepared.

Copilot Chat / Word
Create a briefing document for [the executive]'s meeting today with the product launch team. Include the attendees and their roles, a short summary of the most recent email exchanges between the executive and this group, the open action items from their last meeting with this group, and three smart questions the executive could ask.

4 Draft on their behalf

Your leader asks you to send a note to the leadership team. You draft it in their voice.

Outlook / Copilot Chat
Draft an email on behalf of [the executive] to their leadership team. It should thank everyone for their work closing out the quarter, ask each person to send their top three priorities for next quarter by end of day Friday, and let them know these will be reviewed together at the next staff meeting. Keep it warm, appreciative, and under 150 words.

5 Coordinate the travel

There's an upcoming site visit. You pull the details together into a clean itinerary and catch anything still unconfirmed.

Copilot Chat
Search the inbox for [the executive] for the most recent email about their next business trip, and pull out the key details: dates, flight times, hotel, and any onsite agenda. Then turn it into a clean, organized itinerary I can share with the executive, and list anything that still looks missing or unconfirmed.

6 Close the loop: action items

Yesterday's staff meeting produced commitments. You make sure nothing slips.

Copilot Chat / Teams
From [link the staff meeting notes], create a table of every action item with its owner and due date. Then draft a short, friendly follow-up message I can send to each owner reminding them what they committed to and when it's due.

7 Wrap up: tee up tomorrow

End of day. You set yourself up so tomorrow starts on the front foot.

Copilot Chat
Build me a prioritized to-do list for tomorrow using my own open emails, [the executive]'s calendar for tomorrow, and any action items still outstanding from this week. Put the most time-sensitive items at the top, and flag anything I'm waiting on from someone else.
Your turn: swap in your own details — a real meeting name, a real project — and watch how much more specific the results become. The Agents 101 tab shows how to turn these into a one-click assistant.
Chiefs of Staff

Running the leadership operating rhythm

These prompts walk through a chief of staff's week — synthesizing what matters, running the leadership meeting, turning information into decisions, and communicating outward. Run them in sequence to see the full operating rhythm, or grab any single prompt.

Before you run these: anything in [brackets] is where you drop in a live link — type / in Copilot and pick a file, a meeting, or a person. A couple of prompts mention [the executive] — that's simply who the summary or message is for. The rest work over your own email, Teams, and files.

1 Monday: synthesize the week

You start the week by cutting through the noise — what moved, what's at risk, what needs a decision.

Copilot Chat
Summarize the most important updates, decisions, and risks from my email and Teams messages over the past week, organized around our top strategic priorities. For anything flagged as a risk or a blocker, note who owns it and what decision or help is needed.

2 Prep the leadership staff meeting

The weekly leadership meeting is the heartbeat of the team. You build a tight, decision-focused agenda.

Copilot Chat
Help me build the agenda for this week's leadership team staff meeting. Start from the open action items in [link last week's meeting notes], add the top two or three decisions we need to make this week, and propose time allocations so everything fits in 60 minutes. Format it as a clean agenda I can paste straight into the meeting invite.

3 Synthesize the business review

A stack of QBR material just landed. Your leader needs the signal, not the noise — on one page.

Copilot Chat / Word
Review the quarterly business review materials in [link the QBR file] and write a one-page executive summary for [the executive]. Organize it into what's on track, what's at risk, and the three things that need leadership attention this quarter. Keep it tight and decision-oriented, and call out any numbers that moved significantly versus last quarter.

4 Read the status data

You track a portfolio of initiatives. You want the health picture and anything that just slipped.

Excel / Copilot Chat
Analyze the initiative status data in [link the spreadsheet] and tell me how many initiatives are green, yellow, and red, broken down by department. Highlight any initiative that slipped from green or yellow to red since last month, and give me a two-sentence summary I can share with leadership.

5 Draft the executive message

Priorities for the half are set. Your leader needs to communicate them clearly and credibly.

Copilot Chat / Outlook
Draft a message from [the executive] to the broader organization announcing our operating priorities for the second half of the year. It should connect the priorities to our strategy and sound confident and motivating without being corporate, at around 300 words. Give me one version for email and a shorter version I can adapt for a Teams post.

6 Build a decision brief

A decision is coming up. You frame it cleanly so leadership can decide fast and well.

Copilot Chat / Word
Create a one-page decision brief for the proposal to consolidate our two project-reporting tools into one. Include the background and why this is coming up now, the options we're considering, the key pros and cons of each, the cost and change-management implications, and a clear recommendation with the reasoning behind it.

7 Recap and follow through

The meeting's over. You send a crisp recap so decisions and owners are unambiguous.

Copilot Chat / Teams
Using the transcript from [link today's leadership team meeting], write a concise recap I can send to the team. Lead with the decisions that were made, then list the action items organized by owner with due dates, and close with anything we explicitly parked for a future discussion.
Your turn: point these at your real priorities, your real QBR, your real meeting. Then see the Agents 101 tab to package this whole rhythm into a reusable Chief of Staff agent.
Agents 101

Build your own Copilot agent — no code required

An agent is a custom version of Copilot you set up once and reuse forever. You give it a name, a job description, and instructions for how it should behave — and optionally point it at trusted files or sites for knowledge. Agent Builder lets you create one just by describing it in plain language.

1 Why build an agent?

Consistency

Your best prompt, captured once. Everyone on the team gets the same high-quality help.

Speed

No re-typing long instructions. Open the agent and go.

Grounded

Point it at the right SharePoint sites or files so answers use your team's source of truth.

Shareable

Build it once, then share it with your peers across the leadership office.

2 How to build one in Agent Builder

  1. Open Agent Builder. In Microsoft 365 Copilot, choose Create agent (New agent) to launch the builder.
  2. Describe it in plain language. Paste a description like the starter prompts below. Copilot fills in the name, description, and instructions for you.
  3. Refine conversationally. Tweak the tone, add a rule, change the name — just keep typing what you want.
  4. Add knowledge (optional). Point it at SharePoint sites, files, or other approved sources so it answers from your team's content.
  5. Add starter prompts. Give users one-click example prompts so they know what to ask.
  6. Test it. Try it in the preview pane on the right and adjust until it feels right.
  7. Create & share. Save the agent and share it with the colleagues who'd benefit.

4 The starter prompts we used to build today's agents

Paste either one into Agent Builder's "describe your agent" box to recreate the agent from the demo, then make it your own.

Agent for Executive Admins
Create an agent called Executive Office Assistant that helps executive administrators supporting the Ford leadership team. The agent should help me draft polished, professional emails and calendar invitations in a warm executive tone, create meeting briefing documents that pull together attendees and their roles, recent context, and open action items, turn meeting notes into clear action-item trackers with owners and due dates, and build prioritized daily and weekly task lists. It should always be concise and protect the executive's time, maintain strict confidentiality, use a discreet and professional tone, and ask a clarifying question whenever a request is ambiguous instead of guessing. When I ask for a draft, give me a ready-to-send version plus one or two alternatives for tone.
Agent for Chiefs of Staff
Create an agent called Chief of Staff Companion that supports chiefs of staff working with the Ford leadership team. It should help me synthesize updates from email, meetings, and documents into crisp executive summaries, build leadership meeting agendas complete with action items and time allocations, create one-page decision briefs that lay out the background, the options, the trade-offs, and a clear recommendation, draft executive communications in a confident and motivating voice, and track the status of our strategic initiatives and flag risks early. It should think strategically and stay concise, always organize output with clear headings, distinguish facts from recommendations, and proactively surface the decisions and risks that need leadership attention. Use a crisp, executive-ready tone.
Pro tip for the workshop: after the agent is created, add a few starter prompts (use the Day-in-the-Life prompts from the EA or COS tabs) and connect a knowledge source — like your leadership team's SharePoint site — so the agent answers from your real documents.
