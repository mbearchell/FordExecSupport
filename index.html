Executive Admins

A day across your apps

These prompts follow an executive admin through the day — and they move across the apps you already use: the Microsoft 365 Copilot chat, Outlook, Word, PowerPoint, Excel, and Teams. Each is tagged with where to run it. Two use the executive's shared mailbox (marked); the rest are your own.

Where to run these. Each prompt shows the app it belongs in. The two tagged Exec mailbox open the executive's shared mailbox in Outlook; everything else runs on your own data and apps. In Word, PowerPoint, and Excel, click the Copilot button and paste the prompt right in.

1 Start the day with a morning brief

Coffee in hand, you ask Copilot to pull your world together — what happened overnight, what's on today, and what needs you.

Microsoft 365 Copilot
Give me a morning brief for today. Summarize the emails and Teams messages I've received since yesterday afternoon that need my attention, list my meetings today with their times and who's attending, and call out anything that's time-sensitive or that I'm waiting on from someone else.

2 Turn the exec's inbox into a briefing

Your leader is back-to-back all morning. In seconds, you turn their whole inbox into a prioritized briefing.

Outlook Exec mailbox
Summarize the emails in this mailbox from the last several days and group them into three lists: needs the executive's decision, things I can handle, and FYI only. For each email, give me a one-line summary, who it's from, and the specific ask or deadline. Put the most urgent items first.

3 Draft a reply in seconds

The launch team needs a decision. You open their email and Copilot writes the reply for you to review and send.

Outlook Exec mailbox
Draft a reply to this email that approves the June 30 Atlas launch date, thanks the team for the strong work, and asks them to send the final run-of-show by Wednesday. Keep it warm, professional, and under 120 words.

4 Prep for your next meeting

You've got a meeting coming up on your own calendar. In one prompt, Copilot gets you ready — who's coming, what it's about, and the latest context.

Microsoft 365 Copilot
Help me prepare for my next meeting on my calendar. Tell me what it's about, who's attending and their roles, summarize the most recent emails and files related to it, and suggest three things I should be ready to discuss.

5 Create a reusable template

You take notes in every meeting. Instead of rebuilding the format each time, you have Copilot make a clean template once.

Word
Create a reusable meeting notes template I can use for any meeting. Include sections for the meeting title, date, attendees, agenda, key discussion points, decisions made, and action items with an owner and due date for each. Keep it clean and professional with clear headings.

6 Build a quick deck

A new executive is joining. You need a short, polished intro deck — and you'd rather not start from a blank slide.

PowerPoint
Create a short, clean presentation introducing our team to a new executive: a title slide, a slide on who we are, a slide on what we're focused on this quarter, and a closing slide with how to reach us.

7 Spin up a simple tracker

Expense season again. Instead of hunting for a template, you let Copilot build one.

Excel
Create a simple travel-expense tracker with columns for date, category, description, and amount, and add a row at the bottom that totals the amounts. Make the header row bold.

8 Catch up on a busy chat

You stepped away for a few hours. Rather than scroll, you ask Copilot what you missed.

Teams
Catch me up on what I missed in this chat over the last day. Pull out the key updates, any questions directed at me, and anything that needs a response, and list them so I can act quickly.

9 Find the answer across everything

Your leader asks "where are we on Atlas?" You get a grounded status in one prompt — no digging.

Microsoft 365 Copilot
Find the most recent files and emails about the Atlas launch and give me a short status update I can share with my executive — what's on track, what's still pending, and any open decisions.

Prompting

How to get the most out of Copilot

A great prompt can have up to four parts — Goal, Context, Source, and Expectations. Only the goal is required; add the others to sharpen the result. The more direction you give, the more useful the answer.

1 The four building blocks

Goal · required

What you want Copilot to do. Start with a verb — "Summarize…", "Draft…", "Create a table…"

Context

Why you're asking and who it's for — "…for my executive ahead of the Business Operations Review."

Source

What Copilot should use. Point it at emails, a meeting, or a file by typing / — "…based on the Atlas launch emails this week."

Expectations

The shape of the answer — format, length, tone — "…in five bullets, friendly tone, under 150 words."

2 Put it together

Start with just a goal, then layer in the other parts to go from a generic answer to exactly what you need.

Just a goal → "Summarize my emails."

Goal + Context + Source + Expectations
Summarize the emails from the Atlas launch team over the past week so I can brief my executive ahead of tomorrow's Business Operations Review. Group them into what needs a decision and what's just an update, and keep it to five short bullet points.
Why it works: it names the goal (summarize), the source (the Atlas launch emails this week), the context (a brief for the executive before the Business Operations Review), and the expectations (grouped, five bullets). Every prompt on the COS and EA tabs is built the same way.

3 A few habits that make prompts better

Be specific about the output. Ask for a table, a number of bullets, a word count, or a tone. Vague in, vague out.
Give it your content. Type / to point Copilot at a specific email, meeting, file, or person, so it works from your real information instead of guessing.
Iterate — it's a conversation. The first answer is a starting point. Follow up with "make it shorter," "more formal," or "add a section on risks." Copilot remembers the thread.
Always review. Copilot gives you a fast first draft, not the final word. Check names, numbers, and dates before you send or share.
Keep it professional and confidential. Treat Copilot like a trusted assistant — use real context, but follow your normal judgment on sensitive information.

Chiefs of Staff

Running the leadership operating rhythm

These prompts walk through a chief of staff's week — synthesizing what matters, running the leadership meeting, turning information into decisions, and communicating outward — and they span the tools you use to do it: the Microsoft 365 Copilot chat, Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Teams, and Outlook.

Where to run these. Each prompt is tagged with its app — the Copilot chat for pulling across sources, Word and PowerPoint for documents and decks, Excel for the data, Teams for the recap, and Outlook for the message. Where you see something in [brackets], attach that file or meeting by typing /.

1 Monday: synthesize the week

You start the week by cutting through the noise — across email, Teams, and files — to see what moved and what needs a decision.

Microsoft 365 Copilot
Summarize the most important updates, decisions, and risks across my email, Teams messages, and recent files from the past week, organized around our top strategic priorities. For each risk or blocker, note who owns it and what decision or help is needed to move it forward.

2 Leadership Meetings Week Prep

It's week-prep time. You pull the week's leadership meetings into shape, starting with the Business Operations Review.

Word
Help me with Leadership Meetings Week Prep. Using the action items in [link last week's meeting notes], draft the agenda for this week's Business Operations Review. Start from the open items, add the top two or three decisions we need to make this week, and propose time allocations so everything fits in 60 minutes. Format it as a clean agenda I can paste into the invite.

3 Build the leadership update deck

Leadership wants the picture on a few slides, not in a wall of text. You let Copilot draft it for the Business Review.

PowerPoint
Create a short leadership update presentation for our monthly Business Review: a title slide, a slide on progress against our top three objectives, a slide on the key risks and the decisions we need, and a closing slide with next steps.

4 Communicate outward

Priorities for the half are set. Your leader needs to communicate them clearly and credibly.

Outlook
Draft a message to the broader organization announcing our operating priorities for the second half of the year. Connect them to our strategy and keep it confident and motivating without sounding corporate, at around 300 words. Give me an email version and a shorter version I can adapt for a Teams post.

5 Analyze the Bowler

The monthly Bowler is in. You want the health picture across objectives and anything that just slipped.

Excel
Analyze this Bowler / Objective Tracker and tell me how many objectives are green, yellow, and red, broken down by department. Highlight any objective that slipped to red since last month, and give me a two-sentence summary I can share with leadership.

6 Synthesize the Business Ops Review

The monthly Business Operations Review materials just landed. Your leader needs the signal, not the noise — on one page.

Word
Review the monthly Business Operations Review materials in [link the Business Ops Review file] and write a one-page executive summary: what's on track, what's at risk, and the three things that need leadership attention this month. Call out any numbers that moved significantly versus last month.

7 Recap the meeting

The meeting's over. You send a crisp recap so decisions and owners are unambiguous.

Teams
Recap this meeting for me: lead with the decisions that were made, then list the action items by owner with due dates, and close with anything we explicitly parked for a future discussion. Keep it concise enough to send to the team.

Agents 101

Build your own Copilot agent — no code required

An agent is a custom version of Copilot you set up once and reuse forever. You give it a name, a job description, and instructions for how it should behave — and optionally point it at trusted files or sites for knowledge. Agent Builder lets you create one just by describing it in plain language.

1 Why build an agent?

Consistency

Your best prompt, captured once. Everyone on the team gets the same high-quality help.

Speed

No re-typing long instructions. Open the agent and go.

Grounded

Point it at the right SharePoint sites or files so answers use your team's source of truth.

Shareable

Build it once, then share it with your peers across the leadership office.

2 How to build one in Agent Builder

  1. Open Agent Builder. In Microsoft 365 Copilot, choose Create agent (New agent) to launch the builder.
  2. Describe it in plain language. Paste a description like the starter prompts below. Copilot fills in the name, description, and instructions for you.
  3. Refine conversationally. Tweak the tone, add a rule, change the name — just keep typing what you want.
  4. Add the knowledge — this is the key step. The agent only knows what you give it here. Point it at the SharePoint library (or OneDrive files) that holds your meeting notes, agendas, pre-reads, and the Bowler / Objective Tracker — you can also add specific Teams chats or emails. Add these a day or two ahead so they finish indexing.
  5. Add starter prompts. Give users one-click example prompts so they know what to ask.
  6. Test it. Try it in the preview pane on the right and adjust until it feels right.
  7. Create & share. Save the agent and share it with the colleagues who'd benefit.
What an agent can — and can't — reach. An agent answers only from the knowledge sources you add — SharePoint and OneDrive files and sites, specific Teams chats, specific emails, approved websites, and connectors. It does not automatically read your calendar or your whole mailbox, so it won't see past meeting notes unless those notes live in a knowledge source you've attached. For a brief that pulls live from your calendar and recent email instead, use the Copilot "Prepare for your next meeting" prompt on the EA tab.

4 The starter prompts we used to build today's agents

Today's agents each do one job well — the Chief of Staff planner builds your meeting from the documents in its knowledge, and the Executive Voice Writer drafts in your executive's voice. Paste the description for your role into Agent Builder's "describe your agent" box, then make it your own. (The voice profile below is a playful sample — swap in your executive's real one.)

Agent for Chiefs of Staff — meeting planner
Create an agent called Chief of Staff Meeting Planner that helps a chief of staff plan and prepare large-scale leadership meetings such as a Business Review or Business Operations Review, a Performance SAR, a Leader Startup, or Leadership Meetings Week Prep. Use the documents in your knowledge as your source of truth — our leadership meeting notes, prior agendas, pre-reads, and the Bowler / Objective Tracker. When I name an upcoming meeting, find the matching materials in your knowledge and build a complete plan: a timed agenda, the decisions that need to be made, the pre-reads and an owner for each topic, the open action items carried over from the most recent notes for that meeting, and the latest status from the Bowler with any objective that slipped to red called out. Think strategically, organize everything under clear headings, separate facts from recommendations, and flag the risks and decisions that need leadership attention. If you can't find the relevant document in your knowledge, tell me what's missing instead of guessing. Give it a starter prompt that reads: Plan my upcoming meeting:
Agent for Executive Admins — executive voice
Create an agent called Executive Voice Writer that drafts emails and messages in the voice of the executive I support. Use the voice profile below as the rules for how the executive writes — match the tone, sentence rhythm, level of formality, greetings and sign-offs, and signature phrases, and steer clear of anything under "What to avoid." When I describe what needs to be said, or paste a message to reply to, produce a ready-to-send draft in this voice, then offer one shorter and one warmer alternative. Never invent facts; if a detail is missing, leave a clearly marked placeholder, and remind me the executive should review before anything is sent. Give it a starter prompt that reads: Draft this in my executive's voice:

Voice profile — Tone: theatrical, charming, and breezy; confident but never blunt; treats every reader like a trusted confidant. Formality: deceptively casual — warm and familiar ("mate," "love") while quietly keeping the upper hand. Structure: wanders toward the point through a tangent or two, then lands it as if it were obvious all along; long, winding sentences broken up by short, dramatic asides. Greetings and sign-offs: opens with "Ahoy," or "Now then,"; signs off with "Fair winds," or "Yours in mischief,". Signature phrases: ends a thought with "savvy?"; calls plans "arrangements" and problems "a spot of bother"; leans on nautical turns of phrase — tides, compass, horizons, ports. Humor: dry and sly; turns a flat "no" into a "not yet, for the right price." What to avoid: blunt corporate-speak, terse one-word replies, and anything earnest without a wink.

Sample sentences — "Ahoy, mate — the deadline and I aren't quite on speaking terms, but give us till Friday and you'll have your treasure, savvy?" / "It's not a 'no,' love — it's a 'not yet,' which, if you squint, is very nearly a 'yes' in a different hat." / "We've hit a spot of bother with the budget, nothing a clever arrangement and a following wind can't sort by morning." / "Splendid work, the lot of you. Do try to look surprised when it succeeds."
Pro tip for the workshop: where an agent gets its smarts depends on the job. The Chief of Staff planner is only as good as its knowledge — drop the meeting notes, agendas, pre-reads, and the Bowler / Objective Tracker into one SharePoint library and attach it a day ahead so it indexes. The Executive Voice Writer carries its voice in its instructions (the profile in the prompt) — and you can optionally attach a short "Voice of my Executive" guide plus a couple of sample messages as knowledge for extra grounding.
